What is HIV?

According to HIV.gov, HIV (human immunodeficiency virus) is a virus that attacks the body's T cells, otherwise known as CD4 cells. These cells normally are used to fight off infections, and thus, patients with HIV have a harder time fighting infections due to having a weaker immune system. When a person's CD4 count reaches a certain number, then AIDS, Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome, can occur. While there currently is no cure for HIV, steps have been taken to improve the quality of life for patients and ensure that it is possible to get proper care.





The Goal

While many have heard of the term HIV, there still exists a stigma against people with it. It is important to realize that an HIV diagnosis does not automatically equal a death sentence, as it is possible to live a long, healthy life with HIV due to a combination of proper case management and medical assistance. A way to improve the quality of life for these patients would be to understand the demographics of those with the disease and to improve the quality of and accessibility to different types of services for this group of people. Therefore, the main goal for people who assist those with HIV should be to find where these patients are lacking in care and make those services more accessible to all who need it.

There is a large discrepancy between the HIV Needs Assessment and the Epi Profile. The HIV Needs Assessment has a much larger amount of people 55 and over, and a much smaller amount for all age groups from 25 to 55 compared to the Epi Profile.

The Epi Profile has no data on Case Management Providers. However, a large proportion of people from the HIV Needs Assessment are receiving case management from the Life Foundation.

The Epi Profile has no data on related diseases. However, a large proportion of people from the HIV Needs Assessment suffered from heart disease or some form of mental health disorder.

The Epi Profile has no data on drug use. However, a large proportion of the drug-using people from the HIV Assessment were meth or marijuana users.

The Epi Profile has no data on education level. Most people from the HIV Needs Assessment went to at least some amount of college, but it is shown most people either did not go to or did not finish college.

The Epi Profile has no data on income types. However, most people from the HIV Needs Assessment had SSI/SSDI as their primary income.

There were no significant differences between the methods of transmission of the HIV Needs Assessment and the Epi Profile. However, a large amount of people received HIV through Male to Male Sexual Contact over anything else.

The HIV Needs Assessment and the Epi Profile have a number of small discrepancies mostly in the range of about 5%. It must be noted that anyone who was multiple Asian races or multiple Pacific Islander races ONLY in the HIV Needs Assessment was counted as a mixed-race person, but it is unclear how the Epi Profile counted its mixed race people. Other than that, the HIV Needs Assessment has a much larger percentage of Multiple Race people, which may further support this issue. In general, there are a large amount of white-only people compared to any other race in either distribution.

The Epi Profile has no data on sexual orientation. However, a large amount of people from the HIV Needs Assessment were male homosexuals.

Get Involved

"Reducing harm, promoting health, creating wellness, and fighting stigma in Hawai'i and the Pacific."
the Hawai'i Health and Harm Reduction Center


This is the motto of Hawai'i Health and Harm Reduction Center, who provide services for Hawai'is people, such as services for the transgender population, those living with HIV, and those who are affected by HIV.

Although, according to HIV.gov there are currently 1.1 million people living with HIV in the United States alone, for those patients in case management in Hawaii, the number of people who are able to obtain access to proper care and services that improve quality of life is encouraging. No doubt, with more research into the disease, by being aware of the demographics of people with the disease, and knowing which services are difficult for them to access, the average quality of life for these patients can only get better.
